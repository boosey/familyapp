# Phase 0 + Phase 1 Engineering Spec — The Spine and the Wedge

*Family Chronicle · Build documentation · Companion to the Vision, Release Roadmap, Identity & Data Model, and Consent & Estate Framework. This document is written to be handed to a coding agent. It assumes the strategy docs as context but restates every decision a builder needs so the spec stands alone.*

---

## What this document is, and what it is not

This is the engineering spec for the first two phases of the chronicle: **Phase 0, the spine** — the data model, identity graph, consent ledger, and permission rules everything else reads from — and **Phase 1, the wedge** — the elder voice-capture MVP, where an elder talks with near-zero friction and a family member receives a real story in the elder's own preserved voice, plus the asked-question loop that turns a younger relative's curiosity into the elder's next prompt.

It is precise enough to implement against. It is not a phased calendar, a business plan, or a UI design file. It deliberately stops at the Phase 1 boundary: where the roadmap defers a capability to Phase 2 or later (explore surfaces, external-record enrichment, the steward console, avatars), this spec builds the *seam* — a clean place for the later feature to attach — but does not build the feature. Those seams are called out explicitly so the agent knows what to leave room for and what to skip.

Two decisions were made before writing and are treated as fixed throughout: the elder's single entry channel for the MVP is a **single-tap web link** (not a phone call), and the stack is a **TypeScript-first, managed/serverless architecture with a Python service for the AI pipeline**. Both are justified below.

---

## Part I — Orienting principles a builder must hold

Three principles from the strategy outrank ordinary engineering instincts, and when they conflict with a faster or simpler implementation, they win.

**The elder never feels they are using software.** The entire elder-facing surface is one full-screen page with one primary action. No login, no password, no account, no settings, no navigation menu, no error dialog that demands troubleshooting. If something fails, it fails warmly and silently toward a human (the family member who invited them), never toward the elder. This is not a polish goal; it is the thing Phase 1 exists to prove, and it constrains the architecture: the elder side is a stateless, identity-light, recording-and-playback surface, and all the complexity — accounts, the data graph, permissions — lives behind it on the younger-generation side.

**Authenticity beats polish.** Every story keeps its original audio as the canonical artifact. The synthesized prose is a derived, clearly-secondary rendering that always travels with — and is visibly subordinate to — the recording. The pipeline must never discard, overwrite, or lose the original audio, and the data model treats the recording as the source of truth and the transcript and prose as regenerable derivatives. When the LLM "cleans up" speech into prose, it is editing a copy; the elder's actual words remain retrievable and are what gets foregrounded in playback.

**Consent is owned by the person, not the family, and nothing is shared until the author approves it by voice.** A story exists in a private, unshared state from the moment it is recorded until the elder explicitly approves it. Approval is itself a recorded voice action, and it writes the first entry into the consent ledger. The permission system is not a feature layered on later — it is enforced at the data-access layer from the first line of code, because retrofitting access control onto a system that assumed open access is exactly the "un-retrofittable" mistake Phase 0 exists to prevent.

## Part II — Phase 0: The Spine

Phase 0 builds the atoms and the seams. The guiding decision, locked in the Identity & Data Model doc, is to **separate three things that naive models fuse**: who a person *is* (identity), which families they *belong to* (membership), and what gets *shared where* (visibility). Keeping these as three independent dials is the seam that makes every messy human case — marriage, divorce, blended families, adoption, estrangement, death — an ordinary change of a membership or a visibility setting rather than a structural impossibility. A builder should treat the temptation to collapse Person and Membership into a single "family member" record as the cardinal sin of this codebase.

### The entities

**Person** is the spine. A Person is permanent, singular, and the owner of everything expressive: their stories, their recordings, their voice, and their consent records. A Person exists independently of any family and is never deleted when a relationship ends. Critically, **a Person does not require a login.** An elder is represented as a Person with no associated auth credentials — they are a first-class subject of the system who never authenticates. A younger family member is *also* a Person, but one who additionally has an Account (see below) linked to them. This split — Person as the durable human, Account as the optional, severable login attached to some Persons — is what lets the elder own a rich archive while never touching a password, and lets a person's identity and stories survive long after any login is gone.

Fields a Person carries: a stable identifier; display name and the name the interviewer should speak; optional birth year and other lightly-held biographical anchors used to warm up the interviewer; a `lifeStatus` flag (`living` / `deceased`) that gates posthumous behavior downstream; a pointer to the Account that may control them, if any; and timestamps. The Person owns collections of Stories, Media, and ConsentRecords by foreign key — ownership is on the Person, never on a Family.

**Account** is the optional login attached to a Person. It holds authentication identity (managed by the auth provider — the app stores only the provider's user id and basic profile), and it is the thing a younger relative signs in as. One Account maps to exactly one Person. Most Persons in the early system (the elders) have no Account. This separation matters for a reason that pays off much later: when a person dies, their Account can be deactivated while their Person, stories, and consent records persist untouched and become subject to the story-will — but Phase 0 only needs the seam, which is simply that nothing expressive hangs off the Account.

**Family** (the Chronicle) is a container representing one family's shared record. It has a name, a creator, a created timestamp, and — looking forward — a `stewardPersonId` pointer that in Phase 0 is set to the creator and is the seam for the Phase 4 steward console and succession. A Family owns nothing expressive directly; it is a context that Stories are *surfaced into*, never a container that Stories live in.

**Membership** is the plural, revocable link between a Person and a Family, and it carries a role. This is the entity builders most want to skip and must not. A single Person can have many Memberships (Eleanor is in both the Boudreaux and the Reyes chronicles). Each Membership has a Person, a Family, a `role` (`narrator`, `member`, `steward` — Phase 0 needs these three; the model leaves room for more), a `status` (`active`, `paused`, `ended`), and timestamps for when it began and ended. Divorce ends a Membership; it deletes nothing. Estrangement pauses or scopes one. The Membership's status and role are inputs to every permission check, so a story shared "to the family" is evaluated *per active Membership* at read time — never copied into a family-owned table.

**Story** is the unit of narrative. A Story is owned by exactly one Person (the author/narrator) and is surfaced into zero or more Families according to its visibility setting, evaluated against Memberships. A Story is authored once and never duplicated per family; "sharing" is a visibility computation, not a copy. A Story carries: the owning Person; a `state` (`draft` → `pending_approval` → `approved` → `shared`, plus `archived`); an `audienceTier` (the visibility dial: `private`, `branch`, `family`, `public`); a pointer to its canonical Recording (the original audio Media); the generated transcript; the generated prose rendering; a title and a short summary; auto-extracted theme/entity tags (light in Phase 1); the question that prompted it (and, if it came from a family member's ask, a pointer to that Ask — see Phase 1); and timestamps including the approval time. The recording pointer is required and the prose is nullable and regenerable — the model encodes that audio is primary.

**Media** is any binary asset: an audio recording, and later photos and video. A Media record holds the storage key/URL, content type, duration (for audio), the owning Person, a `kind` (`story_audio`, `approval_audio`, later `photo`/`document`), and a checksum. Approval voice clips are stored as Media too, so the consent ledger can point at the actual sound of the elder saying yes. Media is owned by the Person, lives in object storage, and is never mutated — new versions are new Media rows.

**ConsentRecord** is the minimal consent ledger that ships in Phase 0. Each record is an append-only, immutable event: who consented (Person), what about (a Story, or a scope), the `action` (`approved_for_sharing`, `set_audience_tier`, `revoked`, `paused_membership`), the resulting state, an optional pointer to the approval-audio Media (so consent has a voice, not just a row), the actor who recorded it, and an exact timestamp. The ledger is **append-only**: you never update or delete a ConsentRecord; a revocation is a *new* record that supersedes a prior one. This is the load-bearing decision for everything ethical that comes later — the Phase 4 avatar consent gate, the story-will, time-gated release — all of which read this ledger. Phase 0 writes only the handful of event types the MVP generates, but the table shape and the append-only discipline are built now so nothing downstream has to be retrofitted.

### The relationships, stated plainly

A **Person** owns many **Stories**, many **Media**, and many **ConsentRecords**. A **Person** may have one **Account** (most elders have none). A **Person** has many **Memberships**; a **Family** has many **Memberships**; so Person-to-Family is many-to-many *through* Membership, and the Membership is where role and status live. A **Story** belongs to one **Person** and points to one canonical **Recording** (Media); it is surfaced into the Families its owner is a member of, filtered by its `audienceTier` evaluated against each active **Membership**. A **ConsentRecord** belongs to one **Person** and usually references one **Story**. The mental test for any new table a builder is tempted to add: *does this make a messy human situation ordinary, or does it hard-code one family shape?* If the latter, it's wrong.

### The permission model (built now, enforced from line one)

Visibility is a single dial on each Story with four tiers, and a read is allowed only if the reader's relationship to the story clears the tier. **Private** means author only — the default state of every freshly recorded story, and where it stays until approval. **Branch** means one family line within a chronicle (Phase 0 stores the tier faithfully but a builder may treat branch as equivalent to family for enforcement until branch structure is modeled in a later phase — the seam is the stored value, which must not be lossy). **Family** means every Person with an active Membership in a Family the story is surfaced into. **Public** means open. Every tier is designed to later accept a *time gate* (release on a date, open after death, seal for N years); Phase 0 does not implement time gates but the audience-tier field and the consent ledger are shaped so a time condition can be attached without migration.

The enforcement rule is absolute and central: **all reads of Stories and Media go through one authorization function** that takes the requesting Account's Person (or no Person, for the anonymous elder surface) and the target Story, resolves the Story owner's active Memberships, and returns allow/deny against the tier. There is no query path that returns story content while bypassing this function. Building this as the single front door now is the entire point of Phase 0; it is far cheaper than discovering later that a dozen endpoints each rolled their own access logic.

### What Phase 0 explicitly defers (the seams to leave)

Per the data-model doc, Phase 0 builds the atoms and leaves these seams unbuilt: full **federation** (family-sovereign data hosting — Model C), only relevant at scale; cross-chronicle **conflict resolution and sync**; and deep **multi-lineage genealogy** (the tree deepens in Phase 3 enrichment). A builder should not model these; they should only avoid decisions that would make them impossible — chiefly by keeping ownership on the Person and never letting a Family own expressive content.

## Part III — Phase 1: The Wedge — Elder Voice Capture

Phase 1 is the first thing a real family touches. Its job is to retire two risks: *do elders actually engage*, and *are the resulting stories good enough that families care*. Everything in it serves a single loop — an elder talks, the talk becomes a real story in their own voice, a family member receives it — plus the asked-question relay that keeps the loop self-feeding. The entry channel is the single-tap web link; the interviewer behavior, the elder entry experience, and all consent enforcement are built in-house because they are the IP and the ethical line; transcription, the LLM, and (later) telephony are bought.

### The elder entry experience — the single-tap web link

A younger family member (the initiator, modeled as a Person with an Account) invites an elder by generating a link and sending it via SMS or email through whatever channel they already use. The link encodes a **session token** — a long, unguessable, optionally time-bounded identifier that maps to the elder's Person and the inviting Family context. Tapping the link opens a single full-screen web page. There is no login, no app install, no account creation on the elder's side. This is the literal implementation of "the elder never feels they are using software": the token *is* the identity for the duration of the session, and the page is the entire interface.

The page presents, at most, the warm greeting spoken aloud, a single large high-contrast primary control to start talking, and — during a session — a clear indication that it is listening and an unmistakable way to stop. Type is large and scalable, contrast is high, the layout is linear with no menus, and the only interaction the elder must perform is tapping one button and speaking. Audio is captured in the browser at full wideband quality (a direct benefit of the web-link choice over a phone call: no 8 kHz narrowband penalty, so transcription is more accurate) and streamed or uploaded to the backend. The page tolerates long silences without ending the session, because silence is thinking; the elder ends the session by a single explicit action or by a gentle timeout with a warm close.

Three things make this dignified rather than merely functional. The greeting names the elder and, when the session was prompted by a family member's question, names the asker ("Sofia was wondering…") — warmth and the sense of mattering are features. The same warm voice and persona appear every session so it feels like a relationship. And there is always a spoken off-ramp — "let's skip that," "let's talk about something happier," "that's enough for today" — that the interviewer honors immediately.

*Seam left for later:* the telephony channel (the system calls a landline) is the obvious Phase-2+ second entry channel. The capture pipeline is therefore built to accept an audio stream from *any* source behind a thin adapter, so adding a phone channel later means writing a telephony adapter that feeds the same pipeline — not rebuilding capture.

### The interviewer — the in-house IP

The interviewer is the product's craft and is built, not bought. It is **not a generic chatbot**; it is an oral-history practitioner implemented on top of a bought LLM. The LLM provides language; the interviewer behavior — the rules about *how* to ask, when to stay silent, what to ask next, when to stop — is the in-house system that wraps it. Concretely, the interviewer is a controlled loop around the LLM with a behavior policy enforced in our code and a carefully engineered system prompt, not an open-ended conversation handed to a model.

Its behavioral commitments, drawn directly from oral-history and reminiscence-therapy practice, are these. It asks **open-ended, concrete, non-leading questions** — "Tell me about…", "What was it like when…", never "Don't you think…". It asks **one question at a time** and then stops talking. It is **silence-tolerant**: a pause is not a cue to jump in, and the turn-taking logic waits well past the threshold a normal voice assistant would use. It **reflects and follows tangents** using the elder's own words rather than steering back to a script. It **sequences gently** — simple, non-threatening biographical questions first, sensitive or painful topics only after rapport and only with an easy exit, and it never pushes into pain; it validates, offers to pause or redirect, and (where appropriate) notes that it is not therapy and surfaces that human support exists. It **weights toward the reminiscence bump** — ages roughly 10–30 and first-time, self-defining experiences — because that is where the richest and most identity-defining memories cluster. And it **remembers across sessions**, opening with a warm callback ("Last week you started telling me about the farm and your sister Rosa…") so continuity feels like a relationship rather than a fresh transaction each time.

Mechanically, the interviewer composes each turn from four inputs: a base question set spanning the major life categories (childhood, family of origin, education, work, love and marriage, parenthood, migration, historical events, traditions, faith and values, hardships, hobbies and friendships, places lived, advice and legacy), seeded from the vision's question bank; any **pending Asks** routed from family members for this elder, which take priority and are framed warmly with the asker named; the **session memory** of what this elder has already covered, so it neither repeats nor contradicts; and the elder's lightly-held biographical anchors (birth year, place) that let it "arrive prepared." The behavior policy in our code decides whether the next turn is a fresh base question, a follow-up dig into a rich thread just heard, a routed family Ask, or a wind-down because fatigue was detected — and only then does it call the LLM to phrase that turn in the established warm voice.

### The capture and synthesis pipeline — record → transcribe → speech-to-story, voice always preserved

The pipeline is an asynchronous, staged flow, and its non-negotiable invariant is that **the original audio is the canonical artifact and is never lost or overwritten.** The stages:

**Record.** Audio is captured on the elder's web page and persisted immediately as a `story_audio` Media in object storage, owned by the elder's Person, with its storage key, duration, and checksum recorded. This write happens before any processing, so the recording is safe even if every later stage fails. The Story is created in `draft` state pointing at this Recording.

**Transcribe.** A bought speech-to-text service transcribes the audio. The transcript is stored on the Story as a derived field, with word-level timing if the provider supplies it (timing later enables syncing prose to audio and letting playback foreground the real voice). Transcription accuracy benefits materially from the wideband web-captured audio. The transcript is regenerable — if a better model arrives, re-running transcription is a derivative refresh, not a data change.

Two cheap pre-transcription transforms are applied to a *working copy* on its way into the transcriber, never to the canonical recording. First, **voice-activity detection (VAD) trims dead air** — elders pause a great deal (silence is thinking, and the interviewer tolerates it), but the transcriber should never be handed long silences, which also reduces a known Whisper-family failure mode where the model hallucinates words into quiet stretches. Second, the trimmed speech is **time-stretched to roughly 1.6x** (tempo change that preserves pitch, not a resample). Whisper-class models are robust to moderate speed-up, and ~1.5–1.8x returns near-identical transcripts while cutting audio-seconds proportionally; the safe speed should back off toward ~1.3–1.4x for low-SNR or hard-to-follow audio (heavy accent, weak or breathy elderly voice), and never exceed ~2x, where accuracy falls off a cliff. At current transcription prices (see Build vs. Buy) these transforms are primarily **latency and quality wins, not cost savings** — they get the transcript back faster, tightening the capture→synthesis→approval loop, and feed the model cleaner input. Two guardrails for the implementer: any word-level timestamps come back in sped-up time and **must be multiplied by the speed factor to map back onto the 1x original**; and because some providers bill a per-request minimum (e.g. Groq's 10-second floor), VAD segments should be **stitched into a few longer chunks** rather than sent as many tiny fragments. The working copy exists for exactly this one call and is then discarded; the original audio Media is untouched.

**Speech-to-story.** A bought LLM, wrapped in our prompt and rules, lightly renders the transcript into readable prose — cleaning false starts and filler while **preserving the elder's actual words, idiom, and meaning.** This stage is explicitly constrained against over-polishing: the prose is a faithful, lightly-cleaned rendering, not a literary rewrite, because authenticity beats polish and an LLM left unconstrained will drift from how the person actually speaks. The prose is stored as a clearly-derived, regenerable field, subordinate in every display to the audio. The same stage proposes a title, a one-line summary, and light theme/entity tags.

**Place.** The Story, still `private` and now `pending_approval`, is attached to the elder's archive. Timeline placement and any record enrichment are Phase 2/3 concerns; Phase 1 places the story in the elder's own collection and the family hub list, gated by approval and permission.

Throughout, the audio Media is immutable and primary; the transcript and prose are mutable, derived, and regenerable. A builder implementing this should make it structurally impossible for a synthesis step to write back over the recording.

### The voice-only approval gate — dignity and the first ledger entry

No story is shared until the elder approves it, and **approval is performed by voice**, never by a form the elder must read and click through. In a session — either at the end of telling, or at the start of the next one — the interviewer plays back or reads the rendered story and asks for approval in plain speech: it confirms whether the elder is happy for the family to have it, and lets the elder correct a detail by voice ("1958, not 1959"). The elder's spoken approval is captured as an `approval_audio` Media, the Story transitions `pending_approval` → `approved` → `shared` at the elder's chosen tier, and — atomically with that transition — the system writes the **first ConsentRecord**: action `approved_for_sharing`, referencing the Story, pointing at the approval-audio clip, timestamped. Consent has a voice and a ledger row from the very first story. A correction the elder voices is applied to the prose (a regeneration of the derived field) before sharing; the audio is untouched. This gate is a dignity feature, not a bottleneck — but it is also strictly enforced: the authorization function will not surface a Story to any family member until an `approved`/`shared` state and a backing ConsentRecord both exist.

### The family hub — the minimum payoff surface

Phase 1 ships a **basic** family hub, not the full explore experience (timeline, map, tree, feed, and "ask the archive" are Phase 2). The hub is the younger generation's logged-in surface where they: see the elder's approved stories as a simple list, each playing the **original voice** with the prose shown as secondary; invite an elder by generating and sending a link; and submit questions for the elder (the ask side of the relay). It reads strictly through the authorization function, so a member sees only stories whose tier and the elder's memberships permit. It is deliberately thin — enough to deliver the reward (hearing a real story in a real voice) and to drive the loop (asking the next question), and no more.

### The asked-question loop — the self-feeding relay

This is the engine that keeps the elder engaged between sessions, and it is the cheapest, stickiest trigger because a real human created the reason. It is modeled with one new entity, **Ask**: a question submitted by a family member (the asker Person) for a target elder (Person), with the question text, a `status` (`queued` → `routed` → `answered`), the resulting Story once answered, and timestamps. The flow: a family member submits an Ask from the hub; it is **buffered, never an interruption to the elder**; at the elder's next gentle session the interviewer pulls pending Asks, prioritizes them, and frames each warmly with the asker named; the elder's answer becomes a Story through the normal pipeline and approval gate; on approval, the Ask flips to `answered` with a pointer to the Story, and the answer is delivered back to the asker (a notification in the hub closing their loop). One person's reward is another's signal — the elder's new story is the family's notification, the family's question is the elder's next prompt — and the system runs on the family's own warmth rather than manufactured gamification. Phase 1 builds exactly this relay; the broader trigger catalog (anniversaries, record-matches, mysteries, digests) is Phase 2+ and is left as a seam by keeping Ask one of several possible prompt sources feeding the same interviewer queue.

## Part IV — Build vs. Buy, with named candidate services

The line is drawn exactly where the roadmap draws it: **buy the commodities, build the IP and the ethics.** Buy transcription, the LLM, and (later) telephony — these are well-served by mature vendors and carry no differentiation. Build the interviewer behavior, the elder entry experience, and all consent enforcement — these are the product and the moral position, and outsourcing them would outsource the reason the product exists.

**Buy — transcription (speech-to-text).** Transcription is now effectively a commodity priced in cents per audio-hour, so the choice is about model fit and trust, not cost. *Recommended default:* **Groq — Whisper Large v3 Turbo** (~$0.04/audio-hour), which runs OpenAI's *open* Whisper model on Groq's inference hardware — extremely fast (roughly 200x+ real-time, so a session transcribes in seconds, moving the pipeline bottleneck entirely to LLM synthesis), with word-level timestamps, and the meaningful strategic advantage that because it is canonical open Whisper, a future move to self-hosting is the *same model* and therefore trivial. Groq also offers **Whisper Large v3** (~$0.11/hr) as a higher-accuracy fallback for hard audio and **Distil-Whisper** (~$0.02/hr, English-only) as a budget option; note Groq bills a 10-second-per-request minimum, which is why VAD segments are stitched into longer chunks (see the pipeline). *Configured alternate:* **xAI Grok STT** (~$0.10/hr batch), a *proprietary* model (not Whisper — note the Groq-vs-Grok spelling so the right endpoint, `api.x.ai/v1/stt`, is wired) explicitly tuned for hard telephony-grade audio and entity recognition (names, dates), which may prove more robust on the weakest elderly voices; it cannot be self-hosted. *Further alternates kept in the lineup:* **Deepgram** (low-latency streaming, word-level timing) and **AssemblyAI** (high accuracy, rich async features). Integration point: a single `Transcriber` interface in the pipeline; default to Groq Whisper Turbo and keep Grok STT (and the others) configurable, so early Phase 1 can **A/B them against real elderly-voice recordings** behind a flag and pick by measured transcript quality on the actual hard case. Before sending real elder audio to any vendor, confirm a data-processing agreement you are comfortable with — this is the irreplaceable voice, and "cheapest" never overrides "trustworthy keeper." Wideband web-captured audio plays to all of these.

**Buy — the LLM (synthesis + interviewer language).** Candidates: **Anthropic Claude** (strong instruction-following and a natural fit for the careful, non-leading, warmth-constrained interviewer voice), **OpenAI GPT-4-class models**, and **Google Gemini**. Integration point: a single `LanguageModel` interface used by both the interviewer turn-composer and the speech-to-story renderer, with prompts and behavior rules living in our code, not the vendor's. The model is replaceable; the wrapping behavior is not.

**Buy — text-to-speech for the interviewer's warm voice.** Candidates: **ElevenLabs** (most natural, consistent persona voice — important since the *same warm voice every session* is a dignity requirement), **OpenAI TTS**, **PlayHT**. Integration point: a `Voice` interface; the chosen voice identity is configuration, not code. (Note: this is the interviewer's synthetic voice for asking questions — entirely distinct from the elder's preserved original recordings, which are never synthesized.)

**Buy — auth.** Candidates: **Clerk** or **Auth0/Supabase Auth** for the younger-generation Accounts. The elder side has no auth by design. Integration point: Account stores only the provider's user id; the app never holds passwords.

**Buy — managed data and storage (see stack below).** Postgres and object storage from a managed provider.

**Buy later — telephony.** Candidates when the phone channel arrives in Phase 2+: **Twilio**, **Telnyx**, **Vonage**. Not built in Phase 1; the capture pipeline's source-agnostic audio adapter is the seam.

**Build — the interviewer behavior**, **the elder entry experience**, and **all consent enforcement** (the ledger, the approval gate, the authorization function, the permission tiers). These three are the entire in-house surface area of Phase 1 and where all the engineering care should concentrate.

## Part V — Recommended tech stack, with rationale

The stack is chosen to be pragmatic, agent-friendly (so AI coding agents can move fast with minimal context-switching), and managed/serverless-first (so a small or solo team carries almost no operational burden). It is TypeScript end-to-end for the spine, with a single Python service where Python is genuinely the better tool.

**Language and structure.** A **TypeScript monorepo** (pnpm or Turborepo workspaces) holds the web app, the API, the shared data types, and the interviewer/permission logic. One language across the web surface, the API, and the business logic means an AI coding agent reasons over the whole system without crossing a language boundary, and the data types are shared rather than re-declared — the single biggest agent-friendliness win.

**Web and API.** **Next.js** (App Router) deployed on **Vercel** serves both the thin elder web page and the younger-generation hub, with API routes / server actions for the backend. Serverless by default, near-zero ops, instant preview deploys that pair well with agent-driven iteration.

**Database.** **Postgres via Supabase** (or Neon). Postgres is the right relational fit for the Person/Membership/Story/Consent graph, the append-only ledger, and the permission joins. **Prisma** (or Drizzle) as the type-safe ORM gives the agent a single typed schema that *is* the data model in Part II — the entities map one-to-one to Prisma models, so the schema file is the spec made executable. Supabase additionally bundles auth and object storage if you want fewer vendors.

**Object storage.** Supabase Storage, **Cloudflare R2**, or **AWS S3** for the immutable audio Media. R2 is attractive for zero egress fees on audio playback. The `Media` table stores keys, not blobs.

**Background jobs / the pipeline.** The record→transcribe→synthesize flow is asynchronous and must survive failures, so it runs as durable background work, not inside a request. Use a managed queue/workflow — **Inngest** or **Trigger.dev** (both TypeScript-native, serverless, with retries and step durability) — to orchestrate the stages. Each stage is idempotent and the original audio is persisted before stage one, so any stage can be retried without data loss.

**The AI pipeline service (the one Python piece).** A small **Python (FastAPI) worker** handles the transcription and speech-to-story orchestration, because the speech/LLM tooling, audio handling, and model SDKs are most mature in Python and this is where Python earns its place. It exposes a narrow internal API the TypeScript orchestrator calls, or it subscribes to the queue directly. Everything else stays in TypeScript; Python is scoped to exactly the AI/audio work and nothing more, so the polyglot cost is contained to one well-bounded service. (If you would rather stay single-language to maximize agent velocity, the same pipeline is fully achievable in TypeScript using the vendors' Node SDKs — the Python service is a recommendation, not a requirement, and the interface boundary is identical either way.)

**Cloud provider.** No provider is locked, so the recommendation is a **Vercel + Supabase + Cloudflare R2** triad: each is managed, serverless-friendly, generous at small scale, and avoids standing up raw AWS infrastructure while solo-building. If you later consolidate, all three map cleanly onto AWS primitives. This keeps Phase 0/1 ops near zero and lets the coding agents spend their effort on the interviewer and consent logic, which is where the product actually lives.

**Why this serves the principles.** TypeScript-everywhere keeps the agent fast and the data model honest (the ORM schema *is* the entity spec). Serverless/managed keeps a tiny team out of the ops weeds. The single authorization function and the append-only ledger are plain application code in this stack, easy to test and impossible to bypass if reads are funneled through one module. And the source-agnostic audio adapter plus the swappable `Transcriber`/`LanguageModel`/`Voice` interfaces mean the bought commodities can change without touching the built IP.

## Part VI — Build sequence (by dependency and risk, not calendar)

Sequenced so each step makes the next buildable, and the riskiest assumption is testable as early as possible.

First, **the spine**: the Prisma schema for Person, Account, Family, Membership, Story, Media, ConsentRecord, and Ask; the single authorization function with the four-tier permission check; and the append-only consent ledger writes. Nothing reads story content except through the authorization function — establish that discipline before any feature exists.

Second, **the capture path end to end on the web link**: token-based session entry, in-browser recording, immediate immutable persistence of the audio Media, and the draft Story. At this point an elder can talk and the audio is safe — the cheapest possible test of "will an elder tap a link and speak."

Third, **the pipeline**: transcribe (with VAD-trim and ~1.6x time-stretch on the working copy) and speech-to-story behind the queue, with the original-voice-preserved invariant enforced. Now a recording becomes a readable story while keeping its voice.

Fourth, **the interviewer behavior**: the turn loop, silence tolerance, sequencing, reminiscence-bump weighting, cross-session memory, and the warm voice. This is the IP and deserves the most iteration; build it against real recorded sessions.

Fifth, **the voice-only approval gate**, writing the first ConsentRecord and flipping the Story to shared — closing the consent loop before anything is shareable.

Sixth, **the basic family hub**: the approved-stories list with original-voice playback, the invite-link generator, and Ask submission.

Seventh, **the asked-question relay**: routing pending Asks into the interviewer queue, prioritizing and framing them, and delivering answers back to the asker — closing the self-feeding loop that is the point of the wedge.

When these seven are working for one real family, Phase 1 has retired its risks: an elder engaged with near-zero friction, and the family received a story good enough to care about, in a real voice, safely and consensually. That is the wedge.

## Appendix — Seams left unbuilt (so the agent knows where to stop)

The following are deliberately *not* built in Phase 0/1, but the model leaves clean attachment points for each: branch-level audience structure (tier value stored faithfully now); time-gated release (audience tier + ledger shaped to accept a time condition); the telephony entry channel (source-agnostic audio adapter); external-record enrichment and timeline/map/tree placement (Phase 3 — Story carries light tags and an owner, ready to be enriched); the steward console and succession (Family carries a steward pointer; consent ledger is append-only and ready); the avatar consent gate and story-will (these read the same append-only ledger — which is exactly why it is built correctly now); and the broader engagement-trigger catalog (Ask is one of several prompt sources feeding one interviewer queue). A builder should resist implementing any of these and should resist any shortcut that would make them require a later migration.
