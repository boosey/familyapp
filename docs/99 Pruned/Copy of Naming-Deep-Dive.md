# Naming — Deep Dive: Vellum, Everloom, and the Trunk Metaphor

*Follow-up to Naming-Options.md. Closer look at two finalists plus brandable variations on "the trunk in the attic."*

---

## Vellum — strong meaning, real conflict

**The case for it is still good.** Vellum is the calf-skin surface medieval manuscripts were written on *because it survives a thousand years*. It carries permanence, craft, and "a record meant to outlive us" in two soft syllables an elder can say without effort. It's the best pure-meaning fit you have for "a perpetual chronicle."

**But the conflict is more serious than I first flagged.** Vellum is also a well-known, well-loved **$250–300 book-formatting app** (vellum.pub) used by self-publishing authors to turn manuscripts into finished books. That matters more here than a random same-name company would, because:

- It sits in an **adjacent category** — books, memoirs, publishing. Your product *also* produces books and memoirs from people's words. To a self-publishing author (a meaningful slice of your early-adopter audience), "Vellum" already means something specific.
- It's **strongly associated and actively marketed**, so it likely holds trademark rights in the publishing/software class you'd want.
- The clean `.com` and `.pub` are gone, pushing you to a modifier anyway.

**Verdict:** beautiful name, but I'd treat the conflict as a yellow-bordering-red flag rather than a footnote. If you love it, the realistic path is a **compound that shifts the meaning onto family** and creates trademark distance — e.g. **Vellumkin**, **Vellum & Kin**, **Housevellum**. Those read as "our family's lasting record" and are far enough from a Mac formatting tool to defend. As a bare one-word brand, though, I'd rank Vellum below where I had it.

## Everloom — rule it out

I'd take Everloom off the table. The web check turned up a **crowded field**: an active US trademark ("Everloom — Weaving Everlasting Trust," filed by Everloom LLC), a separate Australian trademark, and tech companies using the name in India and the UK. Even though none is a family-memory product, "weaving everlasting trust" sits close enough to your themes that you'd be fighting an uphill trademark battle from day one. The name was never distinctive enough to be worth that fight. **Drop it.**

If you like the *sound* of Everloom, the salvageable piece is the **-loom** root (weaving = your heirloom-quilt metaphor). That survives in cleaner coinages like **Loomwell** or the trunk-adjacent ideas below.

## The trunk in the attic — turning the image into a brand

You're right that "trunk in the attic" is an image, not a name. The job is to distill *what the trunk means* — a protected vessel where a family stows the things worth keeping and hands them forward — into one ownable word. Four directions, with the literal ones first and the more abstract further down.

**A real-world note that shapes this whole section:** the most obvious trunk words are already working in this exact space. **Trove** is taken twice — a parents'-stories life-capture app (Kickstarter, 2025) *and* a memory-box product for adopted/looked-after children — so I'd avoid "Trove" bare. **Locket** is a 20-million-download consumer photo app. So the winning move is a **trunk word with a twist**, not the bare noun.

### Direction A — the vessel itself (most literal)

- **Cedarwell** *(recommended in this group)* — the cedar chest (what protects quilts and letters from decay) fused with "well," a deep source you draw from. Warmer and more ownable than bare "Cedar," and the cedar-chest = trunk-in-the-attic image is exactly your metaphor. Appears clear in this space.
- **Stowe** — to *stow* something safe (and a real, warm-sounding place name). Short, dignified, easy to say and spell. "Stow your family's story." Some unrelated uses (a law firm, a town) but nothing in family-memory.
- **Cedarchest / The Cedar Chest** — the most unambiguous version; reads instantly as "where we keep what matters." Less sleek, more heartfelt.
- **Steamer** / **Steamerline** — from the steamer trunk that crossed oceans (ties to the immigration/migration thread in your doc). Evocative but "Steamer" alone drifts toward boats and coffee; needs the trunk context to land.

### Direction B — the act of keeping (the trunk as a verb)

- **Stow / Stowe** *(also above — it bridges both directions)*
- **Keep** as a root — a *keep* is the protected heart of a castle *and* the act of preserving. **Keepwell**, **Keepsby**, **Wellkeep**. Gentle, blessing-like, elder-friendly. (Note "Keepsake" is taken; "Keep-" compounds are freer.)
- **Trovekeep** — keeps the treasure-trove warmth but the "-keep" suffix differentiates it from the taken "Trove" and adds the stewardship meaning (your Mode 5). Looks clear.

### Direction C — what's inside the trunk (the contents)

- **Trousseau** — the gathered-and-handed-forward chest (from Options doc). Gorgeous, nearly uncontested, but pronunciation/spelling friction with your elder audience.
- **Reliquary** — the ornate chest built to hold a family's most sacred relics. Stunning, dignified, completely uncontested in this space. Risk: religious/morbid connotation and four syllables; more of an evocative outlier than a daily-use app name.
- **Vestry / Vesta** — *Vesta*, Roman goddess of the hearth and the keeper of the sacred home fire; warm, short, mythic. Ties the hearth and the keeping together. Worth a check.

### Direction D — the attic / the room it lives in (atmosphere, not object)

- **The Attic** is too generic and slightly dusty/negative (forgotten, not cherished) — skip as a primary name, though it's a lovely word for a feature ("up in the attic" = deep archive).
- **Garret**, **Loft** — the attic room, softer synonyms. "Garret" is distinctive but reads bohemian-writer more than family. Minor options.

---

## Where I'd land

On the trunk metaphor, my order is **Cedarwell**, then **Stowe**, then **Trovekeep** — each takes the trunk image and adds just enough twist to be ownable, warm, and elder-sayable. **Reliquary** and **Trousseau** are the beautiful-but-risky outliers worth keeping on the long list for their distinctiveness.

On the two finalists: **Everloom is out** (crowded trademark), and **Vellum survives only as a compound** (Vellumkin / Housevellum) because the bare word collides with a known publishing tool in an adjacent lane.

So if I fold this back into the overall race, the names I'd actually pressure-test next are: **Cedarwell, Mantel, Stowe, Loomwell, Vellumkin** — with **Trovekeep** and **Reliquary** as the wildcards.

*All availability notes are first-pass web/app-store scans, not legal clearance. Run a USPTO class search and live domain check before committing to any finalist.*
