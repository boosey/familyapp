# Naming — Round 3: More Spine, More Distinctive

*Reset on feedback: the earlier names were too soft/sentimental and not distinctive enough. This round trades coziness for confidence and pulls from four fresh wells — voice & oral tradition, lineage & continuity, place & gathering, and abstract/coined. Mix of legible and earned.*

---

## The shift

The first two rounds leaned on the quilt/trunk/hearth imagery, which kept landing soft (Cedarwell, Mantel, Keepwell). The note is clear: **more spine, more apart from the StoryWorth/Heirloom soup.** So these names carry weight, gravity, or strangeness — a name a family says with a little pride, not a coo.

**Already-taken names I'm steering around** (checked this round): *Saga* (funded family-memory app), *Hearth* (family display device), *Trove* / *Locket* / *Heirloom* / *Kindred* / *Keepsake* (all live in this space), *Tellwell* (publisher), *Vellum* (publishing tool). Griot is tied to StoryCorps' program, so I'd avoid it despite the perfect meaning.

---

## Tier 1 — the ones with spine (my favorites)

### Anvil *(strong, legible-ish)*
What gets forged and lasts. An anvil is heavy, permanent, generational — handed down in trades for centuries. It says "this family's record is forged, not fluffy." Hard consonants give it spine; it's the antidote to sentimental. Clear in this space.
- **Reads as:** strength, permanence, craft. A record built to take a hammer.
- **Watch-out:** masculine/industrial lean; less obviously "family." That tension is also what makes it distinctive.

### Recount *(legible, double meaning)*
To *recount* is to tell a story — and a *count* is a reckoning, a tallying-up. So it means both "tell the tale" and "take account of a life." Confident, plain-spoken, a real English word with no babying. Appears open in this space.
- **Reads as:** the act of telling, with a spine of accounting/record.
- **Watch-out:** also means "count again" (elections) — minor, context kills it.

### Loamward *(coined, earned)*
*Loam* is the rich soil everything grows from — roots, lineage, the ground a family comes from. *-ward* means "toward / in the keeping of" (as in *steward*, *ward of*). So it reads as "toward the soil we come from" and "in the keeping of our roots." Invented, ownable, earthy rather than precious. Clear.
- **Reads as:** roots, ground, stewardship — lineage without a family tree cliché.
- **Watch-out:** meaning is earned, not instant; needs a tagline at first.

### Sona *(coined, warm but not soft)*
From *persona / sonus* (sound, voice) — and it simply sounds like a name, warm and human, almost a person you'd talk to. Fits the voice-first, "being listened to" core of Mode 1. Short, global, app-like without being cutesy. Note: a few unrelated "Sona" companies exist (auto parts, research software), none in family memory — worth a trademark-class check but likely clear in your lane.
- **Reads as:** a voice, a presence, someone who listens. (Could even be the interviewer's name.)
- **Watch-out:** verify trademark class; common-ish short name.

## Tier 2 — voice & oral tradition

### Telling
The plain word for the act, but also "a *telling* detail," and "all told." Quiet confidence, no syrup. Possessive works beautifully: *the family's telling.*

### Saga → don't use, but the neighborhood is right
Taken — but it points at the **Norse/epic register**: a family's saga is its long, serious, multi-generational story. Cousins worth a look: **Edda** (the Old Norse book of collected lore and genealogies — distinctive, mythic, short, almost certainly clear in this space) and **Skald** (the Norse keeper-of-stories; harder, more obscure).
- **Edda** is my sleeper pick here: it literally *means* a collected book of a people's stories and lineages. Earned meaning, very ownable, two soft syllables.

## Tier 3 — lineage & continuity

### Scion
A *scion* is the young shoot grafted onto old rootstock to continue a line — and a descendant of a notable family. Elegant, a little aristocratic, very much about continuity. (Note: "ScionPC" is old genealogy software, and Toyota's Scion is retired — worth checking, but a different class.)

### Ember
The live coal carried from one fire to start the next — the literal image of passing warmth/continuity across generations. Short, warm but with heat, not soft. Common word, so an `-` compound or modifier may be needed (`Emberline`, `Emberward`).

### Throughline
The narrative spine that runs through everything — which is *exactly* your "one chronicle underneath, many modes on top" thesis. Modern, confident, a touch more product-y. Compound is long but meaningful.

## Tier 4 — place & gathering (with more edge)

### Homestead
The family's claimed ground and home built to last and be inherited. Sturdy, American, unsentimental. More spine than "hearth." Common word; check availability / compound.

### The Long Room / Longhouse
A *longhouse* held an extended family or clan under one roof across generations — the literal architecture of your product. Distinctive, a little mythic, clearly "many people, one shelter, over time." **Longhouse** is the more brandable single word.

### Vesta
Roman goddess of the hearth who keeps the sacred home fire — warmth + duty + permanence in one short, uncommon word. More mythic spine than "hearth," and far more ownable.

---

## Tier 5 — abstract / coined & ownable

For names built to trademark clean, where meaning is fully earned (the Remento play):

- **Loamward** *(also Tier 1)* — roots + keeping.
- **Sona** *(also Tier 1)* — voice + presence.
- **Sonder** — the realization that every passerby has a life as deep as yours; reframed for family, it's the feeling your product creates when a descendant meets an ancestor's full inner life. Beautiful, knowing, modern. (Coined by The Dictionary of Obscure Sorrows; some scattered use — check.)
- **Orla / Orin / Wren-type human names** — giving the chronicle a *person's* name (the keeper) is an untapped, warm-but-not-soft route; e.g. an interviewer/brand called **Orin** or **Vesta**.

---

## Where I'd point you

If you want **spine and surprise:** **Anvil**, **Edda**, **Longhouse**.
If you want **confident-but-legible:** **Recount**, **Scion**, **Throughline**.
If you want **coined and ownable:** **Loamward**, **Sona**, **Sonder**.

My three to pressure-test next: **Edda** (mythic, means exactly "a people's collected stories," very ownable), **Recount** (plain-spoken double meaning, no syrup), and **Anvil** (pure spine). All three sidestep the soft/sentimental trap and stand well apart from the competitors.
