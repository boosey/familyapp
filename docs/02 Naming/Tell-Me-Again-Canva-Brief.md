# Tell Me Again — Brand & Content Brief for Canva

Paste this into Canva (or any writing assistant) as the brand brain for marketing copy, social posts, landing heroes, and email headers.

---

## Product identity

| | |
|---|---|
| **Brand name** | Tell Me Again |
| **Domain** | tellmeagain.app |
| **Refrain / spoken phrase** | “Tell me again.” |
| **Tagline** | Family voices, kept for the people who come next. |
| **Category** | Private family memory / oral-history product (voice-first) |

**One-line definition**  
Tell Me Again helps families record and keep the stories of the people they love — in their own voice — before they’re gone.

**Slightly longer**  
A private home for your family’s voices. Not another photo dump or family tree. A place to gather the stories only your relatives can tell — how your grandparents met, the house on Plank Road, the year everything changed — and keep them safe for the people who come next.

---

## What it is (and isn’t)

**It is**

- A warm, private family archive of *spoken* stories
- Voice-first: people talk; we listen, transcribe gently, and keep the original recording
- Built so elders (and anyone who’s “not good with apps”) never have to learn software
- Ongoing — a living family record, not a one-time gift project that ends when the book ships
- Private by default; the family decides who hears what

**It is not**

- A genealogy / DNA / family-tree product (though stories can sit beside photos and people)
- A social network or public feed
- A chatbot that “plays” a deceased relative or invents new words in someone’s voice
- Homework for Grandma (no accounts, apps, or setup for the person telling the story)
- StoryWorth-style “write a chapter a week” writing prompts

**True competitor (emotional)**  
The unlabeled shoebox in the attic — and the one relative who “knew all the family history” and took it with them.

**Category competitors (for contrast, not copy)**  
StoryWorth, Remento, HereAfter AI — usually one-time memoir gifts. Tell Me Again is the *kept, shareable, voice-true family archive* that keeps growing.

---

## How it works (core loop — three steps)

1. **Ask** — Send someone you love a question (“Tell me about your first job”) by text or email.
2. **They talk** — They answer out loud, in their own voice, from any phone. No account, no app, no setup.
3. **It’s kept** — We gently transcribe it and tuck it into the family’s private archive, to hear again anytime.

**Photos, too**  
Upload from a device or pull from Google Photos and attach to a memory. A photo often pulls the story out of the person who was in it.

---

## Who it’s for

Write for these people (roles, not ages — roles rotate over a lifetime):

1. **The narrator** (often an elder) — Happy to talk; doesn’t want to fuss with a computer. Wants to feel listened to, stay author of their own story, and leave their voice for grandchildren.
2. **The initiator / organizer** (often adult child) — “Mom won’t be around forever and nobody’s writing this down.” Buys it, sets it up, sends questions, needs proof it’s working without nagging.
3. **The curious grandchild / audience** — Wants surprise and connection: voice, photos, short stories — not a wall of text or a museum assignment.
4. **The steward** — Quietly tends who belongs, what’s shared, and that the archive outlives any one person.

**Primary emotional buyer:** the middle generation racing a clock they can’t see.  
**Primary story source:** the narrator who must never feel they’re “using software.”

---

## Brand voice

**Tone in three words:** warm · dignified · plainspoken

**Sound like**

- A careful relative who asks good questions and waits for the answer
- Oral history, not tech marketing
- Clear, concrete, human (“the house on Plank Road,” “the laugh, the pause”)

**Do not sound like**

- Startup hype, growth jargon, or “AI-powered platform”
- Baby-talk, slang, or condescension toward elders
- Grief-bot / immortality / “talk to Grandma forever” vibes
- Guilt (“before it’s too late!!!”) — urgency is fine; pressure is not
- Corporate privacy theater — be simple and direct about “yours, and only yours”

**Register**  
A grandmother should be able to say the brand name with pride. Dignity first; warmth second; cleverness last.

## Key messages (use / remix freely)

**Hero / lede**  
Tell Me Again helps your family record and keep the stories of the people you love — in their own voice — before they’re gone.

**What it is**  
A home for your family’s voices.

**How it works**  
Three steps. Nothing for them to learn.

## CTAs that fit the brand

Prefer: Start your family · Sign up · Sign in · Ask · Listen · Keep this one · Read our Privacy Policy

Avoid: Unlock · Supercharge · Get started free forever · Don’t miss out · Claim your legacy (too salesy/grand)

## Content do’s and don’ts

**Do**

- Center *voice* — hearing someone tell it again
- Use specific, domestic details (first job, wedding, old kitchen, road trip)
- Emphasize zero effort for the storyteller
- Mention privacy as a core promise, not a footer afterthought
- Keep sentences short enough for older eyes and mobile layouts

**Don’t**

- Lead with AI, transcription tech, or feature lists
- Promise immortality, resurrection, or synthetic “conversations with the dead”
- Position as homework, journaling challenges, or photo dumping
- Use purple-tech / neon / hustle aesthetics
- Overload the first frame with stats, badges, or feature chips

---

## Visual direction (for Canva layouts)

**Feeling:** heirloom, not SaaS — paper warmth, calm pace, stories as the hero; chrome recedes.

**Typography cues**

- Serif for story headlines / emotional lines (memoir feel — e.g. Newsreader-like)
- Clean sans for UI / body
- Mono only for years, places, small labels if needed

**Color / atmosphere**

- Warm paper / cream grounds (not flat white sterile)
- Single accent; soft sage or terracotta possible — avoid default AI purple gradients
- Warm brown shadows, never cold gray chrome
- Optional moods: *heirloom* (default warm), *archive* (cooler museum), *hearth* (soft intimate)

**Imagery**

- Real family moments, hands, phones mid-call, listening — not stock “happy diverse family smiling at laptop”
- Photos that look lived-in: kitchens, porches, old albums, road trips
- Prefer one strong image over collage grids

**Motion / energy (if Canva animates)**  
Quiet. Soft fades. One gentle “listening” pulse at most. No bounce, sparkle, or confetti.

## Proof points you can state (accurately)

- Stories stay private unless the family chooses to share
- Original voice is preserved (hearing *how* it was told matters as much as the words)
- Narrators don’t need an account or app — personal link / phone
- Photos can be uploaded or imported (Google Photos) to spark and illustrate memories
- Content is not sold and not used to train third-party advertising or AI models
